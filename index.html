ITERATIVE DESIGN



Designing intuitive and effective UIs requires a thorough process that includes research, user testing, receiving feedback, and revising designs. In this project, our group of four explored how we could create an interface for KIWI, a Y Combinator-accelerated startup that allows mobile users to send and receive music to each other’s home screens. We did not look at the current interface design until our version had been completed.

Our process was as follows:
  • Initial sketches, prototypes, and wireframe
  • Creation of high fidelity, interactive prototypes
  • Revision of prototypes based on peer feedback
  • Submission of the final design to user testing

SKETCHING AND WIREFRAMING

The premise of this startup is pretty simple. Kiwi is a mobile app that allows users to send songs to their friends’ homescreens. We designed our prototype for a mobile app that has a homescreen widget. Users of all ages will be impacted by our interface, particularly users who are interested in music and the social aspect of sharing music with friends. We anticipate users will want an easy interface that makes the process of sending, receiving, and exploring music simple and fun.

To get ideas flowing, our group created four total initial designs, each consisting of three screens of our choice. This gave us twelve total initial sketches for us to work with:

HIGH-FIDELITY MOCKUP AND CRITIQUE


HI-FI FIGMA

We translated our wireframe into a high-fidelity prototype using Figma. We incorporated our style design guidelines, and adhered to a minimalist color scheme with a few pops of color.



CRITIQUE

During our critiquing session, we received the following feedback:

Font size → the font size in our initial hi-fi prototype was too small and difficult to read. It would be helpful to make use of various font weights to emphasize the intention and hierarchy of each section (particularly in the sending and viewing of songs). Furthermore, for text of \similar importance (headers vs subheaders vs descriptions), we could adjust the font size to be uniform throughout.
navigation →the navigation buttons at the bottom of the screen appeared too big and bulky. We could make it more sleek and modern-looking.
Icon accessibility → we used an airplane icon to symbolize sending Kiwis, but this caused some confusion, as airplanes are not universally known to indicate sending messages. We could add descriptions to each of the navigation icons to limit ambiguity.
Affordances → initially, pressing the “settings” icon on the Profile page allowed the settings to slide in from the right. Then, when pressing the back button, the original Profile page slid from the right again. We could change this so that pressing the settings icon and back button would allow the Settings page to slide in and out, imitating the action of swiping in a page and then swiping it away.
App intention → it was important for us to consider the audience of the product that we were designing. Who is Kiwi intended for, and what is the functionality of it? We aimed to make the app appear like a social media platform for songs.

USER TESTING

SCENARIO

Our prototype is a mockup of a startup called Kiwi. Kiwi is a mobile app and widget that enables users to send Spotify songs to their friends’ homescreen (the most recent song received will display on a user’s Kiwi widget). Note: this is not a real mobile app; instead, we created a prototype of this on Figma. To simulate tapping the smartphone, use your mouse to click on the smartphone. Moreover, if you click on the phone screen, “tappable” (i.e. if you click on them it will take you to a different screen) sections will appear highlighted in blue.

For this test, imagine you are a Spotify user hoping to send a song to your friends. Please feel free to think aloud while interacting with the prototype.



TASK
  1. Open the Kiwi app. Navigate to the send tab. Find Mark Zuckerberg’s profile. Send him the song “August” by Taylor Swift.
  2. Open the Kiwi app. Navigate to your own profile, and open the settings page.


QUESTIONS

  • What were your overall thoughts on this prototype?
  • What improvements would you make to this prototype?
  • Did you run into any difficulties when using the prototype?
  • On a scale of 1-10 (1 being not intuitive at all, 10 being very intuitive), how intuitive was the prototype? How would you improve the intuitiveness of the prototype?



SUMMARY OF FINDINGS

Users were able to complete both tasks fairly quickly and easily and noted that our app’s layout and design was very intuitive.


For task one, many of the users had trouble finding the Kiwi app on the homescreen. They were looking for an app that said Kiwi and eventually decided the widget was what they were meant to click. One user made a good point that the widget looks very similar to any other music widget, such as Spotify or Apple Music, so perhaps in the future, we could improve this by adding a Kiwi logo to the widget as well. Opening the app was the most difficult part, and then users were able to easily navigate to the send page, noting that the labeled icons on the bottom were useful. Once on the send page, users selected the friend Mark Zuckerberg, which brought them to a select song page where they selected and sent the song August. Most users were confident they completed this task correctly, especially since a confirmation pop-up message appears once the Kiwi is successfully sent.
For task two, users were also able to easily navigate to their profile page and then find the settings button in the upper righthand corner. Again, users mentioned that the intuitive icons aided in the process, even though the settings icon wasn’t labeled “settings”, the use of the common gear icon for settings made the page easy to find. One user thought that the profile page shouldn’t be on the bottom with the other options, but rather in the top right corner. They noted that the home, send, and search pages all relate to interactions with other users, whereas the profile page is slightly different because it's only about you. This user originally clicked home when looking for the profile page before noticing the profile button and clicking on that.


Overall, users found the tasks simple and easy to complete, enjoyed the layout of our design and the idea of the startup. Based on their feedback, we definitely will add a more recognizable logo to the homepage widget so the app is easy to find on the homescreen, and we will also consider rearranging the navbar and moving the profile page.
